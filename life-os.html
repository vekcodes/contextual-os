The 5 Hard Rules

Rules, not goals. Goals require motivation. Rules are automatic. You either follow them or you don't — there's no negotiation with yourself at 11pm.

01
First hour of the day belongs to your product, not your clients
Once you open WhatsApp or email, your brain is in reactive mode. You'll spend the rest of the day responding to other people's priorities. The build block is sacred — it's the only time of day you're building your future rather than servicing someone else's.
If a client calls at 9am, let it ring. Call back at 10.
02
10pm is the absolute communication cutoff
You've been sleeping at 3am because of late client tasks. That's not dedication — that's a boundary problem. A professional relationship with boundaries is more sustainable (and more respected) than one where you're available at all hours. Set the expectation once and hold it.
Autoresponder text: "Thanks for reaching out — I'll pick this up first thing tomorrow."
03
Gym is 3x per week, booked in advance, non-cancellable
You don't wait until you feel like going. You have it in your calendar like a client call — Mon, Wed, Fri at noon. If a client wants a call during that slot, you move the client, not the gym. Physical energy is the foundation. Without it, your productivity, creativity, and mood all suffer within 2 weeks.
Block Mon/Wed/Fri 12–1pm in your calendar right now.
04
Sunday 30-min review — every single week
This habit alone eliminates most Monday chaos. Review: What did you ship this week on the AI platform? What 3 tasks matter most next week? Which client calls are coming? Where are your free blocks? Writing this down on Sunday means your brain isn't trying to figure it out Monday morning while clients are already pinging you.
Use the Weekly Review section below. 30 minutes, Sunday evening.
05
When you feel lazy: do exactly 5 minutes, not zero
The lazy feeling is real — and it's often your body and brain recovering from accumulated stress, poor sleep, and decision fatigue. Fighting it with willpower doesn't work. Instead: open the project, pick the single smallest next action, do it for 5 minutes. 80% of the time you'll continue. The other 20% — 5 minutes is still 5 minutes more than zero, and momentum compounds.
Keep a "5-minute tasks" list for each project — things so small they feel stupid. Use them as entry points.

Client Management Protocol

You're an independent contractor, not an employee. That means you set the terms of engagement. Most clients will respect professional boundaries when stated calmly and upfront — not defensively.

Do this
  • Set response hours in writing: "I respond 10am–6pm Mon–Fri"
  • Batch check messages 3x a day — not constantly
  • Acknowledge tasks within 2 hrs during work window
  • Push back on scope creep clearly and professionally
  • Block client calls in advance on a shared calendar
  • Use a project management tool (Notion, Linear) for task tracking
  • Review all 5 clients in one 15-min morning triage
Stop doing this
  • Responding to messages at midnight
  • Allowing tasks to arrive with no deadline or scope
  • Keeping phone notification volume on all day
  • Doing client work before your own build block
  • Taking new tasks with no agreement on timing
  • Working with 5 clients at equally high intensity simultaneously
  • Feeling guilty for not being available 24/7
CLIENT AUDIT

With 5 clients, be honest: which ones generate the most revenue relative to time spent? Which ones message at midnight most? Which ones consistently have unclear scope? The ones draining you more than they pay you are worth replacing with fewer, better clients — or raising their rates significantly.

When You Feel Like Doing Nothing

This is your exact protocol for days when you can't get started. Don't fight it — route through it.

1
Name what's happening — don't gaslight yourself
Are you tired? Anxious? Overwhelmed? Bored? Each has a different fix. "Lazy" is a symptom, not a character flaw. Identify the actual thing — say it out loud or write it.
2
If you're tired → sleep or take a 20-minute nap. Not a TV show
A real nap (set an alarm for 25 min) often restores more than an hour of half-productive scrolling. Give your brain actual rest.
3
If you're overwhelmed → write every open loop on paper
Your brain can't hold 5 client threads + 2 products + family + finances simultaneously. The overwhelm is real. Write it all down — every pending thing. Then pick ONE thing and close everything else.
4
Open your AI platform folder and do one small thing — exactly 5 minutes
Read a file. Rename a function. Write one line of documentation. Something so small it's almost embarrassing. The point isn't the output — it's that you showed up.
5
If 5 minutes doesn't turn into more, close it and go for a walk
A 20-minute walk without a podcast or phone does more for tomorrow's productivity than trying to force another hour today. You can't think your way out of burnout.
6
At night: identify what tomorrow's first 5-minute task is and write it down
Before bed, decide the one smallest entry point for tomorrow morning. When you wake up, you're not figuring out where to start — you already know. Reduces the resistance to zero.

Anatomy of a high-performing LinkedIn post

1
Hook line (first 2 lines — everything)
People see this before "see more." Make it a bold claim, a surprising number, a relatable frustration, or a direct promise. "I managed 5 clients and built a product this week. Here's what actually happened." beats "Excited to share my journey."
2
Body — short paragraphs, white space
1–2 sentences per paragraph. No walls of text. Use line breaks generously. LinkedIn is scanned, not read — make it easy to skim and still get value.
3
The insight or payoff
One clear takeaway. What should the reader do, think, or feel differently? Don't bury it — it should be obvious and quotable.
4
Closing question or CTA
Ask a question to drive comments. "What's your biggest GTM challenge right now?" Or a soft CTA: "Follow for weekly build updates." Comments boost distribution far more than likes.
Formats that work for you
  • Story posts — "This week I…" with a real lesson
  • Numbered lists — "5 things I learned building GTM for [industry]"
  • Before/after — "Client's pipeline before vs 60 days after"
  • Build-in-public updates — weekly, honest, specific numbers
  • Hot takes — a contrarian view in your niche
Avoid these
  • Motivational fluff with no specific insight
  • Posting links (LinkedIn suppresses external URLs)
  • Long unbroken paragraphs
  • Tagging people who aren't relevant
  • Posting and disappearing — comment on others' posts too
